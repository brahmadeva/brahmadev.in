In the earliest Vedic traditions, Brahma was revered universally as the unmanifested supreme reality brought forth into creation. This period marks the apex of his distinct worship, where he was recognized not merely as a creator, but as the foundational consciousness from which all existence emanates.

The Rigveda's Nasadiya Sukta (10.129) captures this primordial understanding: before the universe was differentiated into the familiar triad of creator, preserver, and destroyer, there existed a singular, undivided principle. Brahma, identified with Hiranyagarbha (the Golden Womb), was that principle made manifest—the first self-aware being to emerge from the void.

## Hiranyagarbha: The Golden Womb

The concept of Hiranyagarbha (हिरण्यगर्भ) represents perhaps the most profound contribution of early Vedic thought to human understanding of cosmic origins. Described in the Rigveda (10.121), Hiranyagarbha is the "golden embryo" that floated in the void before creation, containing within itself all the potential of the universe.

This concept is not merely cosmological but deeply philosophical. Brahma as Hiranyagarbha represents the transition from pure, undifferentiated Brahman to the manifest cosmos—the first act of self-recognition by the Absolute.

## Archaeological Evidence

Archaeological findings from the Indus Valley Civilization period suggest that creator deity worship was widespread in the Indian subcontinent long before the composition of the classical Puranas. Seal imagery depicting four-faced deities has been interpreted by scholars as early precursors to Brahma iconography.

The prevalence of fire altars (yajnavedi) in Harappan sites aligns with Vedic descriptions of Brahma as the lord of yajna—the cosmic sacrificial fire through which creation is sustained.

## Textual Sources

Primary sources for this period include:

- **Rigveda** (1500–1200 BCE): Hiranyagarbha Sukta, Nasadiya Sukta
- **Atharvaveda** (900–700 BCE): Skambha hymns identifying the cosmic pillar with Brahma
- **Shatapatha Brahmana** (800–600 BCE): Detailed cosmogonic narratives featuring Prajapati-Brahma
- **Aitareya Brahmana** (600–400 BCE): Descriptions of Brahma's role in priestly tradition
