description: The Shri Brahma Suktam, drawn from the Atharva Veda and the Taittiriya Brahmana, glorifies Brahman as the Supreme Being, the first-manifested source from whom the gods, the Vedas, and the whole universe arise. Revered as the inmost Self and the foremost of all beings whom none can rival, it is chiefly chanted in the Udakashanti rite performed before the Upanayana and other samskaras.

extraInfo: "The Brahma Sūktam is found both in the Atharva Vēda and the Taittirīya Brāhmaṇa. This text deals with the glory of the Supreme Being. The Brahma Sūktam is used during the famous ritual Udakaśānti. The Udakaśānti is very prevalent and well known to many. It is performed before Upanayana where Varuṇa is invoked in the waters and later the same water is poured on the people who are getting their Upanaya done, this is the same for all the Samskāras. Some even do daily Pārāyaṇa of this Mantra, but normally it is used mainly in the rituals.  "
significance: <p>The Brahma Suktam, found in both the Atharva Veda and the Taittiriya Brahmana, extols the glory of the Supreme Being, Brahman, as the primordial source from whom the gods, the Vedas and the entire universe arise. It opens with the well-known mantra Brahma jajnanam prathamam purastat, describing Brahman as manifesting first, before all.</p> <p>As a Vedic suktam it invokes Brahman as the origin, support and inmost Self of all beings, the one none can rival. It is especially associated with the Udakashanti rite performed before the Upanayana and other samskaras.</p>
benefits: <ul><li>Invokes the supreme Brahman as the origin of the gods, the Vedas and the worlds.</li><li>Confers the sanctifying merit of Vedic recitation and the peace of Udakashanti.</li><li>Purifies and blesses the waters and the participants in samskara rituals.</li><li>Cultivates contemplation of Brahman as the inmost and highest reality.</li><li>Draws well-being, strength and auspiciousness through its concluding oblations.</li></ul>
recitationMethod: <p>As a Vedic mantra it should be chanted with proper accent and clarity, ideally as learned from a qualified teacher. It is principally used in ritual, notably the Udakashanti, where Varuna is invoked in the waters that are then poured over those undergoing a samskara.</p> <p>Some devotees also take up its daily parayana; when so recited it is best done after purification, in a calm and reverent frame of mind, as a contemplation of the Supreme.</p>
viniyog: <p>This suktam is drawn from the Atharva Veda and the Taittiriya Brahmana, its deity (devata) being Brahman, the Supreme Being; it is chiefly employed in the Udakashanti and in the samskara rites, and may also be recited daily as a hymn to the Supreme.</p>
